The problem with lagging signals

The standard CS warning system is built on lagging indicators: a missed QBR, a drop in product usage, a spike in support tickets, an unresponsive champion. By the time these surface, the customer is already somewhere between frustrated and decided. Your team isn't catching risk. It's documenting it after the fact.

The intervention math is stark. A save play executed 90 days before renewal has a meaningfully higher success rate than one executed 30 days out, which is better than one after a churn notice. Yet most teams are structured, consciously or not, to trigger action at the late stage. The tools flag what's already broken. Nobody is watching what's about to break.

At Suki AI, I inherited a $3.2M ARR portfolio where churn was being detected at the "unhappy customer" stage rather than the "activation gap" stage. Building a framework that caught at-risk signal gaps in Salesforce during the first 90 days of deployment shifted intervention timing from reactive to predictive, and drove clinical activation from 24% to 59% across the portfolio.

The core shift is from monitoring outcomes to monitoring trajectories. Outcomes are what happened. Trajectories are where things are heading. Early signal architecture is built on trajectories.

The five signal categories

Churn doesn't have a single cause. It has a pattern. Across 9 years and two clinical AI companies, I've found that meaningful risk always shows up across five observable dimensions, each with both leading and lagging indicators. The leading indicators are the ones worth building your intervention logic around.

Risk scoring and intervention triggers

Signal detection without a scoring system creates alert fatigue. The goal is a composite risk score that tells your team where to intervene, in what order of priority, and with what motion. Below is a three-tier intervention framework with score ranges and corresponding actions.

Tier 1 — Healthy (70-100)

Profile: On-track activation, strong champion engagement, utilization at or above contracted level.

Focus: Expansion readiness. Begin identifying expansion signal and initiating value conversations.

Scheduled QBR cadence. Expansion playbook initiated at month 4 if utilization >85%.
Tier 2 — Watch (40-69)

Profile: One or more leading signals below threshold. Trajectory is degrading but relationship is intact.

Focus: Targeted intervention within 7 days. Do not wait for a lagging signal to confirm the risk.

CSM-led diagnostic call. Identify root signal. Executive sponsor loop-in if champion engagement is the trigger.
Tier 3 — At Risk (<40)

Profile: Multiple high-weight signals below threshold or a single critical signal (champion departure, stalled activation) confirmed.

Focus: Executive-led save play within 48 hours. Assign a dedicated recovery plan with 30-day milestones.

Leadership escalation. Cross-functional recovery plan. Success plan reset if onboarding is the root cause.

Implementation notes

Building this architecture from scratch requires three prerequisites before you touch tooling. First, a segment definition with a clear average TTV and activation milestone map for each segment. Without a benchmark, there is no trajectory to measure against. Second, a CRM that captures activity data with enough fidelity to detect engagement patterns, not just closed/won stages. Third, a documented success plan template that ties customer milestones to the signal thresholds above.

At Pointcare, this architecture lives inside a multi-signal customer journey model in Salesforce that surfaces risk scores automatically. At Suki AI, I built an earlier version of it manually, using a combination of Salesforce activity fields and a weekly scoring review. Both versions produced meaningful early churn reduction. The tooling matters less than the discipline of defining the signals, setting the thresholds, and closing the loop between signal and intervention.

One practical note for regulated environments: your signal architecture needs to be designed with audit defensibility in mind. Every intervention trigger should have a documented rationale, and every save play should be logged with timestamps and outcome notes. In healthcare SaaS, this isn't optional. It's also good practice. Teams that document intervention logic improve it faster than teams that don't.

The goal of this framework is not zero churn. It's earlier churn, where you have the most leverage, the lowest intervention cost, and the highest probability of a successful outcome. Start with two or three high-weight signals, set your thresholds, and build the habit of weekly review before you automate. The system gets better the more consistently it's used.
